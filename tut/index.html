For years, the British archaeologist考古学家 Howard Carter had searched the Valley of the Kings in Egypt, convinced that one royal tomb still lay hidden beneath the sand. By 1922 his patron, Lord Carnarvon, was ready to stop paying, and Carter was almost forced to give up. Then, in November, his workers uncovered a step cut into the rock. It led to a doorway still sealed密封 with the ancient古代的 plaster of more than three thousand years before. On it was a royal name: Tutankhamun, a boy king who had died at about eighteen. Carter made a small hole and held up a candle. What the candlelight revealed揭示 was astonishing. Unlike almost every other royal tomb, which robbers had emptied long ago, this one was very nearly intact完好无损的. Inside lay a remarkable非凡的 collection of treasures珍宝: a golden throne, chariots, jewellery, and, at its heart, the solid-gold burial mask that would become the most precious珍贵的 face of ancient Egypt. The discovery gave the world its clearest window yet into how the pharaohs had lived — and died. A boy who ruled for only a few years had, in the end, told us far more than many kings who reigned for decades.
