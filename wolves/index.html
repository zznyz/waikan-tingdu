How Wolves Changed the Rivers of Yellowstone

For most of the twentieth century, no wolves howled in Yellowstone National Park. Hunters had killed the last of them by the 1920s, and for about seventy years the park went without its top predator捕食者. Without wolves, the elk multiplied大量繁殖 and grazed freely, stripping the young willows and aspens that grew along the rivers. The bare banks crumbled into the water, and many streams turned wide, shallow and lifeless. Then, in 1995, biologists released释放 a small number of grey wolves brought down from Canada. The wolves preyed on the elk, and the survivors幸存者 grew warier than before. Fewer and warier of ambush, the herds no longer lingered in the open river valleys. Freed from constant grazing, willows and young trees finally rose again beside the water. Beavers returned to feed on them, and their dams created ponds where fish and birds could thrive茁壮成长. Roots bound the soil, the banks grew firmer, and the streamsides slowly came back to life. Scientists call this kind of chain reaction a trophic cascade, when a change at the top of the food web reshapes重塑 the living world below. The full story is still debated争论, yet Yellowstone reminds us how deeply a single species物种 can be woven into a whole living system.
